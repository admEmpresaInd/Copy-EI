Introduction
A Prehistoric Fable
The Copywriter and the Club
Once upon a very old time (it might have been in the 20th century), when direct
marketers wore loincloths and copywriters scratched out advertising messages
on stone slabs, developing good sales copy was a primitively brutal process.
To bring home the brontosaurus one day, an enterprising young copywriter
(Let’s call him Rex) trekked to the tribal village (Let’s call it the Village of Ud)
to apply for his first assignment. There the tribal chief (Let’s call him
Humongous) gave Rex a challenge: Beat the control, and you will be rewarded
with copious victuals, impressive trinkets, and worthy praise.
Duly tempted, Rex asked, “And what, pray tell, do you mean by ‘beat the
control’?”
“Simply write a sales message that’s better than what has been written
before,” Humongous told him.
“And if my writing only equals the strength of the previous messages?”
“Then we will let you try again,” Humongous said.
That seemed like a square deal to Rex, so he took the assignment. “What
shall I write about?” he then asked.

---
<!-- Página 9 -->

“Tell of my greatness and the wonderful things I have done,” Humongous
said.
“And what are the wonderful things you have done?” Rex asked.
Humongous glared at him. “It is your job to know.”
So Rex picked up the small tablet on which he had written “greatness” and
“wonderful things,” and journeyed back to his cave in the hinterlands.
There he toiled for six weeks, scratching out praises for Humongous and
inventing stories of his many wonderful deeds. When he was done, he carried his
tablets back to the Village of Ud and presented them to Humongous and his
counselors.
After reading Rex’s work, Humongous smiled and pronounced his efforts
“Good.” Rex’s heart warmed to hear that word — but before he could thank him,
Humongous added, “However, there are just a few things I think you should
change.”
Tablet in hand, Rex readied himself to make the recommended changes. But
with each suggestion he gave, Humongous hit him with a club.
“Why are you beating me?” Rex asked.
“Don’t be a ninny!” Humongous scolded. “I club you only to drive the
point home. How can you expect to be a great copywriter without a good
clubbing now and then?”
With each strike of the club, Humongous’s counselors applauded. “Ah yes,
Humongous,” they said. “Such wise advice! And so forcefully given! For this
lesson that Rex is getting, he should be paying you!”
Feeling that Humongous has indeed done him a great service, Rex
journeyed back to his cave, nursed his injuries, and spent another two weeks
making the suggested changes. When he returned to Ud, Humongous wasn’t
there. In his place was Fastidious, Humongous’s chief counsel.
Fastidious read the revised copy and pronounced it “Good.” But, he added,
“there are just a few more things you need to change.”
And so Fastidious began the process of telling Rex what was wrong with
his copy — and with each suggestion, he would give him a good knock on the
head. Six hours later, battered and bloody, a dazed and confused Rex returned to
his cave with a stack of new tablets filled with corrections.

---
<!-- Página 10 -->

There, he nursed his wounds while he scratched out the words Humongous
had suggested and put in their place the new words that Fastidious had beaten
into him. When he returned to Ud several weeks later, he was confident that he
had finally nailed the assignment.
This time, Humongous was back — and when Humongous read what Rex
had done, he gave him a whole new lesson in clubbing.
“Why do you beat me so?” Rex asked.
“I beat you to teach you,” Humongous answered. “It is the system handed
down from the ancient masters. Do you dare to dispute it?”
Hungry to feed his family, Rex submitted once again to the beatings. And
once again he went back to his cave and made the new changes.
This pattern repeated itself for a period of six months. Meanwhile, Rex and
his family were starving. Finally, the day came when Humongous, perhaps too
tired to bestow yet another beating, announced that the copy was finished.
Everyone applauded.
Rex had hoped to receive his reward then and there, but was given only a
brontosaurus leg to tide him and his family over. “When we get the results of
your copy we will give you the rest of what you are owed,” Humongous said.
And so, once again, Rex returned to his cave.
Months passed by without a word. The brontosaurus leg was down to the
bone, and Rex’s wife ordered him to go back to the tribal counsel and demand
full payment.
When he arrived at Ud, Humongous was not there. Fastidious greeted him
and told him that he would not be paid anything, because they had decided not to
send it out and test it.
“But why, after all that work, wouldn’t you send it out?” he asked.
“Do not think it was not hotly debated,” Fastidious replied. “Some counsel
members thought it would work well. Others thought it wouldn’t. Finally our
master, the Great Humongous, told us that we had tarried too long on this
project. He has found another copywriter who he feels can get the job done
faster. The new copywriter got his instructions and left the village this morning.
He is very promising!”
“Very promising?” wondered Rex incredulously.

---
<!-- Página 11 -->

“Yes indeed … and you should see how well he took his beating!” said
Fastidious with a smile.
A lesser caveman would have given up at that point. But Rex continued to
come back to the counsel. Gradually, as the years passed, he managed to get
some of his copy sent out and tested. Some of it did beat the controls, and when
it did he was richly rewarded.
But each and every effort involved the same process — going back and
forth countless times and many, many beatings.
Looking back at his career years later, Rex could see no relationship
between how often and hard he was beaten and how often he beat the controls.
When his sons were old enough to leave the family cave and earn their own
brontosaurus meat, Rex advised them to become lyre players or fishermen.

Copy Logic! The New Science of Producing Breakthrough Copy
(Without Criticism)
By Michael Masterson
“Your writing stinks,” the cover note read. It was attached to my first effort at
writing copy. I had spent a full week researching and writing that four-page sales
letter. I wanted it to be good. RJ’s brief critique made me want to either argue
with him or quit writing altogether.
I decided to argue with him. And so he sat me down and went over my
letter, line by line, tearing apart each awkward phrase, ridiculing my highfalutin
language, stopping from time to time to ask me if I wouldn’t be happier working
with numbers instead of words.
That was the tough love I got from my first copywriting mentor back in
1978. “Copywriting takes skill,” RJ told me afterward. “If you want to develop
that skill, you’ve got to be able to take criticism.”
RJ wasn’t the only person who felt that way. Everyone I knew or met in the
direct-marketing business in those days used the same approach.
Back in college, my writing teachers called it the critical method. It was a
centuries-old system for making essays better by breaking them down into their
parts (paragraphs and then sentences and then words) and finding faults with
those parts.

---
<!-- Página 13 -->

Find the faults first, the critical method says, and then fix them. By fixing
the mistakes, the essay becomes stronger.
Since it was the way we learned to write in college, it only made sense that
we would use it in business. And, in fact, most businesses still use the critical
method to evaluate and improve copy. That means that most copywriters still
suffer through the same old process. Perhaps you recognize it:
1. Get an assignment.
2. Do the best you can with it.
3. Have one person or several people read your copy and provide you with
criticism, both specific and general.
4. Revise the copy accordingly, while, at the same time, weighing the
usefulness of the criticism and the power of the person who provided it.
5. Have the revised copy critiqued again by the same or different people.
6. Go back to step four and repeat the process.
This continues until someone in authority says “Stop!”
The critical method works, but it is expensive (in terms of time and money
spent) and unreliable. Sometimes the copy is improved, but not always. It can be
“revised” back to the original draft, or simply discarded as clunky and
unmanageable.
Fortunately, there is a new and better way. That new way is the subject of
this book.
Enter: A New Copywriting Science
For more than 30 years, I’ve had the pleasure of working with some of the
brightest copywriters in the country. During that time, I never missed the
opportunity to learn something from each and every one of them.
In 1993, I began consulting with Agora Inc., one of the world’s largest and
most successful direct-marketing publishers. Working with Bill Bonner, I set
about to assemble the lessons I had learned into a journal of “copywriting
secrets.” Many of these secrets became part of the curriculum that American
Writers & Artists Inc. (AWAI) established when they set up the first
comprehensive program to teach novices how to write breakthrough copy.

---
<!-- Página 14 -->

Using these secrets, Agora and AWAI were able to turn inexperienced
writers into strong copywriters. But the process was long and hard. On average,
it took two to four years — and a lot of hurt feelings — to achieve the
transformation.
The problem, we see now, is that our method of evaluating their copy was
antiquated. It was the old critical method.
That all changed a few years ago when we trashed the critical method and
put in place a new system that we call Copy Logic. Copy Logic has three distinct
parts:
1. The Peer Review — an ingenious way to evaluate the effectiveness of copy
that does not depend on the opinion of one person but has a small group
“beta test” the headline and lead of a direct-response sales letter.
2. The CUB Critique — a super-efficient system for improving everything
that comes after the headline and lead.
3. The Four-Legged Stool Test — a technique that copywriters can use by
themselves to ensure that all their promotions are well balanced.
The Peer Review was created in 2003 at a copywriting retreat in France. Its
first incarnation was rudimentary. But it was vastly better than the critical
method. Master copywriters Mike Palmer, Jack Forde, and I continued
improving the process into what it is today. The method you will learn in this
book is very strong. It will probably change the way you review and revise copy
forever.
The CUB Critique was created in 2007 in Baltimore. It is an amazingly
quick and easy way to improve the second half of the sales letter, which includes
the sales argument and the closing.
The Four-Legged Stool Test was invented much earlier, during the early
1990s in Florida. It ensures that the four essentials for powerful direct-marketing
sales copy are included in any advertising material.
Used together, these three techniques give marketers and copywriters a
revolutionary way to make their promotions more successful. It is so effective
that it deserves its own name. We call it Copy Logic, the new science of
producing breakthrough copy.

If that’s not reason enough to adopt these new techniques, consider this:
You will never have to endure another critical comment — especially from

You won’t spend endless hours having your copy dissected. The total time

If you’re a business owner, you will not have to worry about spending three

If you’re a team leader you will be happy to know that all of your
marketing and creative people are learning the secrets of good copy at the

From Criticism to Creativity
By Michael Masterson
___________________________
“In just 20 minutes we can turn “B”- level copy
into “A”- level copy.”
— Katie Yeakle, Executive Director
American Writers & Artists, Inc.
___________________________
“Now let’s hear some ideas,” he said, standing beside a large chalkboard. “But
there’s one rule. You’re not allowed to make any critical remarks.”
We weren’t sure what he meant by that. But nobody said anything.
“Come on,” Thom said. “Give me a suggestion.”
A hand went up. A recently hired marketing assistant sitting in the back
offered a headline: “How to Cash in on the Technology Boom.” Thom wrote it
on the board. “Okay,” he said. “Let’s have another one.”
One of the senior copywriters raised his hand. “I think it would be better,”
he said, “if that headline were more specific.”
“That’s a criticism,” Thom said. “I need a suggestion.”

---
<!-- Página 19 -->

The senior copywriter flushed. “It is a suggestion,” he insisted. “I’m
suggesting we take that headline and make it more specific.”
Thom smiled. “Okay,” he said, “give me an example of what you mean.”
The senior copywriter thought for a moment. “How about ‘How to Make
450% on the Coming Boom in Cyber Technology’?”
“Now that’s a suggestion,” Thom said. He wrote it down. “Another
suggestion, please.”
One of the new copywriters raised his hand timidly. “I really like that new
headline,” he said.
“Stop!” Thom shouted. “You are making a criticism.”
“I’m saying I like it,” the young man pleaded.
“That’s a criticism,” Thom said. “Positive comments are criticisms too. I’m
looking for copy suggestions. Just copy.”
At the end of the session, Thom opened the floor to a general discussion.
“Can we actually say what we want to say now?” one frustrated senior
copywriter asked.
“Go ahead,” Thom said.
What ensued was a lively melee of opinions. All sorts of theories were
proffered about which headlines were best and why one type of headline might
be better than another. Everyone was having a good time.
Thom’s session was spirited, but we could see that it had two distinct
characteristics. One was creative and the other was chaotic. The creativity
happened when we adhered to the no-criticism rule. The chaos occurred when
we were doing what we were used to — criticizing one another.
It seemed clear to us that nothing useful came out of the arguing and the
criticism. But there were at least a dozen good ideas that were developed when
Thom managed to get the group to follow his rules.
We were skeptical. Thom’s no-criticism approach did seem very New Age.
We were reared in the tough love of criticism. It had seemed normal to us. Even
healthy.
But we couldn’t deny what we had seen: The best headlines were advanced
when we were not allowed to criticize copy, make comments about copy, or say

---
<!-- Página 20 -->

anything except specific copy suggestions.
We went back to our jobs thinking about this experience. We couldn’t help
but wonder if it couldn’t be incorporated into the copy critiques we were doing
every day. Little by little, we attempted to implement the no-criticism rule. Little
by little, we began asking one another to forgo criticism for positive suggestions.
About six months later, we were convinced that there was method to
Thom’s madness. Demanding positive suggestions from ourselves was a better
and faster way to improve copy than dishing out criticism.
But we needed a way to evaluate the copy in a non-critical way too. How
could we explain how we felt about the copy — whether we thought it was good,
bad, or indifferent — if we didn’t use critical language?
We knew that a well-written lead does two things:
1. It stimulates emotional prejudices, and
2. It provokes unconscious feelings.
And we knew that the effectiveness of a lead is determined by how well it
does those two things. That, we realized, was the key. We had to find a way to
quantify the emotional power of a lead.
The answer was a simple one. We would rate our gut reactions on a 4-point
scale. A grade of 1.0 would indicate that the lead created a strongly negative
emotion — a feeling that would make us want to toss the promotion in the
garbage. A grade of 4.0 would indicate a strongly positive gut response — an
urgent feeling that made us want to read on. A grade of 2.0 would indicate a
neutral emotional reaction. And 3.0 would mean a positive reaction, but with
some skepticism.
To some of us, the idea of asking people to evaluate the emotional impact of
a lead by giving it a number grade seemed absurd. But when we tried it out, it
worked very well.
It was actually very easy to indicate the strength of our gut reactions by
assigning numbers. And the more we practiced it, the more natural it felt.
The next step was deciding just how many people should be involved in
these new copy sessions. At first, it was just the copywriter and two experienced
“copy masters.” But then we found that the sessions went faster when we added

---
<!-- Página 21 -->

another person. Eventually, we discovered it was best with four to six people.
With the critical method, it had made sense to limit criticism to those who
had lots of experience with copy. But with this new approach, almost anyone
could participate. We found that even neophytes were capable of rating copy on
a numeric scale, and that their scores were usually consistent with those of
people who had been working with copy for years.
By putting together these three elements — positive suggestions, numeric
evaluations, and a group of four to six people — we had developed what today
we call the Peer Review (PR).
Introducing the Peer Review
The PR is one of the three techniques this book will cover. It is probably the
most important one, because it addresses the most important part of any
promotion: the lead.
For the purposes of this book, I will define the lead to include the envelope
teaser (if any), the headline and any other copy before the salutation (in a sales
letter), and the first one or two pages of body text. Roughly speaking, the lead
usually constitutes the first 200 to 400 words of copy.
Let’s take a look at an example to demonstrate what I mean.
(Note: The example below is an article, not a sales letter, that was written
for AWAI’s e-letter, The Golden Thread. As you can see, the Peer Review
process can be used for both editorial and promotional content.)

Because in a peer review, everybody is engaged in making

As I said, the lead is the most important part of any promotion because it
determines whether the busy reader will continue reading your sales message or
trash it (by deleting it or tossing it) and move on to something else.
When a lead is compelling, the prospect wants to read further. And he does
so with enthusiasm and positive expectations. A compelling lead will make the
prospect want the product that will be introduced to him in the second part of the
sales promotion — whether or not he realizes he is about to be sold.
A well-written lead is extremely powerful, and does most of the work
toward making the sale. A poorly written lead kills the sale. A well-written lead
followed by a mediocre sales argument and close will work. But a poorly written
lead followed by a stellar sales argument and close will fail completely.
And that is why the PR is so important. It is — far and away — the best
way to make any lead stronger.

The Peer Review in Action
By Michael Masterson
________________________________
The peer review process helped bring one strong section of
the piece of copy we reviewed up front to the lead … and the
package turned out to be a blockbuster. The peer review
probably helped generate a few extra million in sales.”
— Justin Ford, Sovereign Society
________________________________
Let’s take a look at how the Peer Review works.
At a scheduled time, four to six people (including the copywriter) assemble
for 30 minutes to review a piece of copy. The copy to be reviewed is the lead
only — not the entire promotion. The lead consists of the envelope teaser (if one
is being used), the headline and any other copy that comes before the salutation
(in a sales letter), and the first 200 to 400 words of copy.
The Peer Review has two purposes. The first is to evaluate the copy. The
second is to improve it.
Everyone in attendance takes part in the evaluation process. And everyone
can take part in the effort to improve the copy. To evaluate the copy, each person

---
<!-- Página 28 -->

rates his gut response to it by scoring it from 1.0 to 4.0. To improve the copy,
each person is encouraged to volunteer suggestions.
In addition, four members of the group have specific responsibilities. One
person tabulates the numerical scores. Another keeps the process on schedule.
The copywriter takes notes. And one person acts as the Peer Review leader.
The Players
1. The Copywriter
The copywriter is always present. His job is to take notes and make on-the-
spot revisions. In agreeing to the Peer Review, he has also agreed to make
whatever revisions are needed within 24 hours. He arrives at the PR with his
own copy of the promotion (double- or triple-spaced is best so he can emend it
manually) and copies for everyone else in attendance.
2. The Leader
One member of the group takes on the role of leader. His job is to run the
review according to the rules.
3. The Timekeeper
Another member of the group becomes the timekeeper. His job is to make
sure the PR moves along quickly and will be completed within the 30-minute
maximum timeframe.
4. The Tabulator
A fourth person, the tabulator, is in charge of marking down the individual
scores and converting them into an average. He brings a handheld calculator to
the PR to speed up his calculations.
In addition to the above, a typical Peer Review might include two more
people who will participate in scoring the copy and coming up with suggestions
to improve it.
The Process
Every Peer Review has the following seven parts:
1. Introduce the copy to be reviewed.

---
<!-- Página 29 -->

2. Evaluate the headline.
3. Improve the headline.
1. Re-evaluate the headline.
2. Evaluate the rest of the lead.
3. Improve the rest of the lead.
4. Re-evaluate the entire lead
1. Introduce the copy.
The leader begins by asking the copywriter to briefly describe the product
that is being sold, its price, and to whom the copy is directed. He then instructs
the Peer Review group to imagine that they are the intended audience, knowing
what the intended audience knows and doesn’t know, and to rate the copy
accordingly.
2. Evaluate the headline.
The review starts with the headline. So that there are no misunderstandings,
the leader specifies what he considers the headline to be. It normally includes the
main headline (which is usually in large, boldfaced type), eyebrow or superscript
heads (copy above the main headline printed in smaller type), and any copy that
follows the main headline and precedes the salutation (often bulleted copy or
copy in a Johnson box).
If some of the PR participants are first-timers, the leader will remind
everyone how the rating system works:
A score of 1.0 indicates that you definitely would not read beyond the
headline.
A score of 2.0 indicates that you probably would not read on.
A score of 3.0 means you probably would read on, but with some doubt or
skepticism.
A score of 4.0 means you definitely would read on with a high level of
interest.

---
<!-- Página 30 -->

The leader also reminds the group that they can extend their scores to one
decimal point: 1.5, 2.8, 3.4, etc.
Then he says: “On a 4-point scale, how do you rate this headline?”
He looks to each member of the group in turn, and they announce their
scores. It might go like this:
First Participant: 2.8
Second Participant: 2.9
Third Participant: 3.5
Fourth Participant: 3.2
As these ratings are given, the tabulator punches them into the calculator,
averages them, and announces the final score. (In the case of our example, it
would be 3.0.)
The copywriter is allowed to rate his own headline. This might seem
counterintuitive — that the copywriter would be biased in favor of his own copy.
But we have found that most copywriters form objective opinions of their own
copy after they have put it away for a while. Giving them the chance to rate their
copy has several interesting psychological benefits. If they are proud of it, they
can announce their feelings via their rating. If they are disappointed in it, they
can sort of apologize by giving it a low rating. Most important, they feel
included in the rating process.
3. Improve the headline.
The leader then asks for suggestions to improve the headline. At that point,
anyone can offer a specific suggestion, such as, “I’d change the phrase ‘cleans
really well’ to ‘cleans like magic.’”
After each suggestion, the leader asks the group to compare the suggested
revision to the original by saying: “Better, worse, or neutral?” The raters are
expected to give quick, gut-level responses. If they want to give a more nuanced
response than simply “better, worse, or neutral,” they can say “strongly better or
worse” or “slightly better or worse.”
The copywriter is not permitted to evaluate the suggested revisions. This
may seem to contradict the fact that he is allowed to rate the headline. In fact, it
doesn’t. When the copywriter rates his copy he is working with the other raters.
When he rates their suggestions, he is rating the raters, as it were. (“Oh, so you
gave my copy a 1.0. Well, let me tell you what I think of your suggestion:

---
<!-- Página 31 -->

Strongly worse!”)
The leader keeps track of the responses to each suggested revision. (“That
suggestion got two neutrals, one better, and one worse.”) The copywriter is
required to make any revision that receives all positive responses. He is likewise
required not to make revisions that receive primarily negative responses. He is
encouraged to make revisions for suggestions that are primarily positive with
one or two neutrals. And he is allowed to decide for himself whether to make
revisions that receive mixed reviews (e.g., two positives, one negative and one
neutral).
Allowing the copywriter some latitude to go with his gut on mixed-
response suggestions reinforces his feeling that he is ultimately in charge of the
copy. One of the major benefits of the PR is that it preserves this feeling of
ownership — which is necessary to motivate the copywriter to revise the lead
according to the best suggestions. With the old critical method, the copywriter
often had the opposite feeling. He felt demoralized because the copy was turning
into something that was no longer his, and this sometimes resulted in poorly
done or never done revisions.
The timekeeper is required to limit the headline review to five minutes so
the group will have sufficient time to review the lead. But the leader doesn’t stop
improving the headline after a single suggestion is made. He continues asking
for suggestions until all new ideas seem to have been exhausted or the five-
minute limit has been reached, whichever comes first.
4. Re-evaluate the headline.
The leader asks the copywriter to read the revised headline back to the
group, and asks the group to rate it again. In most cases, the new rating will be
higher than the original rating. So long as it receives a rating of 3.0 or above, the
leader can move on to the evaluation of the text copy. If it is rated a 2.8 or 2.9, he
can go back to it later, after reviewing the lead, if time permits.
5. Evaluate the rest of the lead.
With the headline finished, the group moves on to the rest of the lead. The
leader reminds everyone that, in evaluating it, they should imagine that they are
the intended audience for this copy.
Usually, a good chunk of the promotion — 600 to 800 words — is
distributed to the group before the actual Peer Review to give them a chance to

---
<!-- Página 32 -->

read more of the promotion than they’re actually going to review. Nevertheless,
the leader gives them a few minutes to scan it and refresh their memories.
______________________
Why the PR Group Should Read More of the Promotion
Than They’re Going to Review
Most prospects don’t have the time or patience to read
through every sales promotion they pick up. Before they
even read the first word, they have usually made a
subconscious decision to read just enough to get a sense of
whether the information will be useful to them. How much
they are willing to read — whether it is 50 words or 500
words — depends on:
(1) the promotion’s size (Generally speaking, the larger it is
the more text they are willing to read before making the
trash-or-continue decision.)
(2) the promotion’s form (Bookalogs generally get more
time than letters.)
(3) the lead (The stronger it is, the more attention the
promotion will be given.)
To get the best results from the Peer Review process, the
leader should require the copywriter to present the group
with at least three to four typewritten pages of copy ahead of
time. Of that, only half of it — the first half — will actually
be rated.
There is a very good reason for providing the group with the
additional copy. Oftentimes, when the lead is mediocre,
there is something better — some sentences or ideas — in
the copy that follows. By moving that better copy up, the
emotional impact of the lead can be greatly improved.

The tabulator tallies up the scores and calculates an average.

If it fails to rate that high, the leader will abort the session. He will say,
“The copy did not meet the minimum score we need, so we won’t review it

copywriter, and will give the copywriter 24 hours to use those suggestions to try
to write a new lead.
Master copywriter Jack Forde, one of the early pioneers of the Peer Review,
uses 2.5 as his cut-off, because he feels he can still find ways to salvage the
copy. But unless you have a master copywriter (like Jack) as the leader, we
found that copy rated below 2.8 is very difficult to improve.
It’s better to ditch the effort and start again from scratch.

6. Improve the rest of the lead.
If the copy scores 2.8 or better, the leader asks for suggestions. Once again,
all of the participants — including the copywriter — are asked (and expected) to
offer suggestions. But, as with the headline, all suggestions must be in the form
of a specific improvement, not a comment or criticism. If someone does attempt
to make a comment or state a criticism, the leader must interrupt him and ask for
“a copy suggestion.”
The success of the Peer Review hinges on the elimination of comments and
criticism. It is imperative for the leader to strictly apply this rule, even if the
comment/criticism is brilliant and/or complimentary.

---
<!-- Página 34 -->

After each suggestion is made, the leader asks the others to compare it to
the original copy and rate it “better, worse, or neutral.” As with the headline
review, participants can be more specific and say “slightly or much better” or
“slightly or much worse.” If the leader notes that someone is hesitating to rate a
suggestion, he can (and should) interpret that as a sign that the participant is
uncertain, say so, and go to the next person.
We have found that it is very important to keep this part of the Peer Review
moving quickly. Speed helps people respond on a gut level. It also keeps the
negativity (“worse”) to a minimum, which maintains the positive attitude that is
so fundamental to this process. To stimulate quick responses, we sometimes
allow participants to shout out their responses (Better! Worse! Neutral!) rather
than asking for them in turn, the way we always do the numerical ratings.
As with the headline review, the copywriter is required to make any
revision that receives all positive responses.
7. Re-evaluate the entire lead.
The leader asks the copywriter to read back the new lead with all the
suggested improvements. Then he asks the group to re-evaluate it.
If it scores 3.0 or better, the copywriter is asked to make all the suggested
changes, as well as any additional changes that the new changes dictate, and
deliver the completely revised copy within 24 hours.
Benefits to the Copywriter
If the Peer Review has been conducted properly, the copywriter will be
excited to get the revisions done, because (a) most of the hard work has been
done for him, and (b) he has experienced for himself how the copy has been
improved … and feels like he owns it.
Most of the PRs we are doing these days begin with a rating of 2.8 to 3.0
and end with a rating of 3.0 to 3.5. (So far, none of the copy has achieved more
than 3.5 after a Peer Review.) But make no mistake: An improvement of two to
five decimal points is a very big deal. At that point, the marketer should get the
copy out as fast as he can. It is a waste of time to try to tweak it further. Just put
it in the mail (or e-mail) and see how it does.

The Peer Review Rules: How and Why You Should Follow Them
By Michael Masterson
________________________________
“You can’t beat peer reviews for honest feedback. If you’re
willing to shut up and listen to suggestions without getting
defensive, your copy can only improve.”
— Jeanie Rainey, Copywriter, Stansberry & Associates
________________________________
You’ve learned how Peer Review works. And when the rules are followed, there
are four benefits you will derive from the process almost immediately.
1. No time will be wasted discussing or arguing about critical theory.
2. You will be able to quickly and easily evaluate the forcefulness of the lead
by using a numeric rating system. (What used to take 10 to 30 minutes to
accomplish with the critical method takes just a few minutes with the PR.)
3. The group will give priority to the most important copy problems — the
problems that can affect response rates.
4. Most important, the Peer Review process stimulates a much higher level of
creative output from everybody involved. Thus better solutions are

---
<!-- Página 50 -->

discovered more quickly.
Those benefits, combined, can dramatically increase the quantity and
quality of good copy that you produce. To enjoy those benefits, however, you
have to follow the rules. These are the six main ones:
Criticisms are not allowed.
Comments are not allowed either.
Only copy suggestions are permitted.
Numerical scores are used to do the evaluation.
The copywriter is always present — and is required to make the suggested
revisions within 24 hours.
Four to six people work together to revise the copy on the spot.
Let’s take a look at each of these rules individually.
Rule #1. Criticisms Are Verboten!
The critical method was, essentially, a negative method. The PR is,
essentially, a positive one.
The power of keeping things positive was evident to us when we first
started working with the Peer Review back in 2003 at that copywriting retreat in
France. We had a hard time refraining from criticism during those initial
brainstorming sessions — but when we did limit our statements to positive
suggestions, a great deal of creative progress was made. And in a very short
amount of time.
So that rule — the old Dale Carnegie prohibition against the “three Cs”
(criticism, condemnation, and complaints) — became the foundation of the Peer
Review.
There are many ways to lodge a criticism. You can be very direct: “The
headline sucks.” Or you can be gentle: “The headline might be improved if we
made it a bit more specific.” You can be oblique: “The headline reminds me of a
crowded bus.” Or you can be very clear: “This headline has too many ideas in
it.” However you dress it up, a criticism is a criticism. And criticism, in a Peer
Review, is prohibited.

<!-- Página 51 -->

In case you have any doubt about what constitutes a criticism, here are a
few more examples of the kind of statements that are not allowed in a Peer
Review:
“I’d like to see a different first sentence — one that isn’t such a cliché.”
“It reminds me of Faulkner, but without the cleverness.”
“There are lots of claims here, but little proof.”

“Copywriters often resent criticisms and critiques,
sometimes justifiably so, because the comments are harshly
worded and often subjective. The peer review process, with
its logical 1 to 4 rating system, takes the emotion out of both
reviewing copy and getting your copy reviewed by boiling
the reader’s response down to a numbered rating.”
— Bob Bly, author and freelance copywriter
___________________
To conduct an effective Peer Review, the leader must be alert to these sorts
of subtle and/or indirect criticisms. When they are made, the leader should cut
them off immediately. “Only suggestions, please,” he might say.
Over the years, we’ve found that Peer Reviews degenerate and lose power
when criticisms are allowed to slip in. Only a diligent posture against them can
keep the system running smoothly.
Why Criticisms Damage the Peer Review Process
Criticism is prohibited because it thwarts creativity on several levels:
First, and most obviously, criticism demoralizes — if only a little — the
inexperienced participants who are the very people that you want to
motivate.
Second, one critical comment often leads to another — especially with
participants who are experienced and/or accomplished. When their
suggestions are criticized they may, in turn, criticize the criticism (or

---
<!-- Página 52 -->

sometimes the critic).
This leads to the third level of creative dysfunction. Arguments can erupt
that, however much they are meant to elucidate and advance the session,
actually derail the creative process and have everybody taking sides or
retreating. Neither reaction is good for developing good copy.
And it’s not just negative criticisms that are a problem. Positive critical
statements, too, are prohibited — statements such as “I thought the headline was
excellent” or “The promise is very strong.”
Positive criticisms can boost the copywriter’s spirits, but that is not the
purpose of the Peer Review. The purpose is to make the copy better. And the
most efficient way to do that, we have found, is to bar any sort of criticism, good
or bad.
Positive criticism hurts the PR process because (a) it takes up time
unnecessarily, (b) it influences other participants to be positive, and (c) it often
provokes comments, which — as I explain in Rule #2 — are also prohibited.
The bottom line: All forms of criticism — direct, indirect, clever, dull,
negative, and positive — are not allowed!
Rule #2. Comments Are Not Allowed Either.
Saying “The headline is weak” is a criticism. Saying “Headlines should be
captivating” is a comment.
Comments, like criticisms, are not allowed in the Peer Review. Why? For
the same reason that criticisms are not allowed. They slow down the process.
They provoke more comments, and can be distracting. Plus, ultimately, they are
completely unnecessary.
Of all the rules we have established, this one is the most difficult for PR
leaders to follow.
PR leaders tend to be experienced marketers or copywriters. Because they
understand copy technically, they often feel that they should educate the group
by, for example, explaining why a particular sentence or paragraph doesn’t work.
That’s a natural and benevolent impulse — but it slows up and often
detours the PR process.

“Last year, our three-person team produced several front-end
controls and more than $12 million in revenue for the
Oxford Club Group. And it’s safe to say we wouldn’t have
accomplished half that without peer reviews.”
— Jay Livingston, The Oxford Club

If you want to allow for comments, you can set aside five or 10 minutes for
them after the PR is completed. But recognize that the purpose of comments is to
educate the participants in direct-marketing/copywriting theory. It has nothing to
do with the purpose of the Peer Review, which is to make the copy better.
Rule #3. Only Copy Suggestions Are Permitted.
Peer Review participants are allowed to express themselves in three ways:
1. By giving the copy a score — from 1.0 to 4.0 —that reflects how
emotionally compelling they found it to be
2. By giving specific copy suggestions
3. By responding to other people’s copy suggestions by rating them better,
worse, or neutral (or — if they want to be more precise — slightly or
strongly better or worse)
Of the above, the only one that is sometimes misunderstood (and hard to
enforce) is the second one — giving only “specific copy suggestions.”
By “specific copy suggestions,” we mean actual phrases or sentences to
replace those in the existing copy.
For example, if the headline of a particular sales letter reads Where the
Rich Keep Their Money, a participant might suggest Where the New Rich Keep
Their Millions as a substitute. He would do so, presumably, because he thinks
the original headline is too vague. That it could be improved with a little more
specificity. But instead of saying “It is a little vague” (which is a criticism) or “It
would be better if it were more specific” (which is a comment), he expresses
those thoughts by translating them into copy: Where the New Rich Keep Their
Millions.
Either this suggestion is preferred by the group or it is not. There is no

---
<!-- Página 54 -->

debate. There is only a vote.
The participant doesn’t have to say that he thinks the copy would be better
if new were added to rich and millions were substituted for money. That is
implied when he suggests Where the New Rich Keep Their Millions.
You can see how the prohibition against comments saves time. For openers,
because the participant won’t be making his point about the value of using
specificity in writing, it will save one to five minutes (depending on how verbose
he is). But it will likely save five times that time by avoiding the lively
discussion such a comment would provoke. Keep in mind; You have four to six
people talking about copy. And at least half of them are writers or marketers.
Give them a chance and they’ll talk about concepts all day!
That’s just what you want to avoid.
The purpose of the Peer Review is to evaluate and improve the emotional
impact of a lead. The most efficient way to do that is to evaluate it numerically
and then make copy improvement suggestions.
The whole job gets done much faster and with much less fanfare if nobody
is allowed to make a comment — good or bad, wise or silly.
Rule #4. Numerical Scores Are Used to Do the Evaluation.
With the critical method, evaluations are often expressed subjectively:
“It is boring.”
“It moves slowly.”
“It’s a cliché.”
Subjective comments feel clever and correct to the person who utters them,
but mean and stupid to the person whose copy is being criticized. And even
when they are presented in the kindest of terms, they tend to be generalizations.
As generalizations, they are usually either confusing or unhelpful.
In rating copy in a Peer Review, you are not asked for your thoughts or
feelings about how “good” or “bad” it is but for a number that represents your
gut reaction to it. Would you want to read on? And if so, with skepticism or
enthusiasm?
By limiting you in this way, you are better able to filter out all sorts of

---
<!-- Página 55 -->

competing thoughts and feelings. You don’t have to think about what good copy
is meant to be. You don’t have to placate the copywriter. All you have to do is
give your immediate emotional response. Does the copy move you? And if so,
how much?
The leader of the Peer Review reminds the group to imagine themselves to
be potential customers for whatever the copy is selling. That doesn’t mean they
need to imagine any thoughts or feelings they don’t have. It means only that they
must pretend to be typical customers who might be interested in such a product.
Taking that one qualification into consideration, the PR participants respond
honestly by rating the copy according to a numerical system that the leader
explains.
He will say, “How do you rate the headline on a four-point scale if:
1.0 means that you would definitely not read any further.
2.0 means that you would probably not read any further.
3.0 means that you would probably read further but with some skepticism.
4.0 means that you would read further with a high level of interest.
That’s the standard. Nothing else needs to be considered.
To make sure their scores are based on an honest emotional response, the
leader should insist that participants make no verbal comments whatsoever —
that they simply give their numeric scores quickly and precisely.
The leader may have to remind the participants not to worry about any little
technical objections they may have with the copy. None of those things matter.
What matters is this: Will the prospective customer read beyond the
headline? And if they do, will they read with enthusiasm or skepticism?
Isn’t that the bottom line?
How much they read. How emotionally open they are when they start
reading. Those are the two most important factors to consider when evaluating a
headline. You can’t evaluate those things very well with subjective criticism. But
you can do it by stating a number.
If you like the copy — i.e., if you feel it is emotionally compelling — you
will give it a high rating, a 3.0 or above. If you have some problems with the

---
<!-- Página 56 -->

copy, you will rate it between 2.0 and 3.0. If don’t like the copy, you will rate it
below 2.0.
You may have many clever thoughts about why you like or dislike the copy.
And you may very well want to express them. But the Peer Review is not a
forum for expressing ideas about copy. It is a technique for improving it.

The numeric rating system is designed to predict how well the copy would
do with potential customers as the lead of a finished sales piece. It will give you

Should You Use Paper Ballots?
Instead of asking participants to give their scores verbally,
you might want to use paper ballots. That way, you eliminate
the possibility that inexperienced participants will feel
intimidated into giving scores consistent with those given by
a more experienced or respected member of the group.
A paper ballot also prevents the possibility that participants
will be influenced by the scores they hear before giving their
own.
_____________________
Quality vs. Responsiveness
The Peer Review is not about the quality of the copy (whatever that is) but
about how responsive prospective customers are likely to be to it.
This distinction is important — but, when we critique copy, it is often
forgotten. Even experienced marketers sometimes forget that the goal is not to
measure the copy against some qualitative ideal but to determine if it will

---
<!-- Página 57 -->

perform well in the marketplace.
Evaluating copy, therefore, is not about whether the grammar and style (not
to mention punctuation and spelling) are correct. Rather, it is about whether the
copy has a strong persuasive appeal.
The numeric rating system is wonderful because it works. And it is
beautiful because it is democratic. Four to six people give ratings. Each rating is
weighted equally. The marketing veteran’s rating is no more valuable than the
brand-new marketing assistant’s.
That’s the way it should be. Because what you are after is not the critical
reaction of a particular marketing professional but the average gut reaction of
thousands of potential consumers.
And that’s why, in a Peer Review, the experienced marketer doesn’t get any
more votes than a beginning customer service representative. Even a 22-year-old
who is working for the first time has at least 10 years of shopping experience to
tap into. In terms of expressing his gut reaction to advertising copy, that person
is just as qualified as the 20-year marketing veteran.
The numeric rating system gives us a way to express the gut feeling of the
average prospective customer that the copy is targeting. And that’s all you want
from a Peer Review. How strong is this lead? Will most prospective customers
be interested in reading on?
We don’t need to know what any one person thinks about why the lead
works or fails to work. What we need is a sum total emotional reaction to it: Did
it work or didn’t it? And if it worked, how strongly?
Rule #5. The Copywriter Must Be There — and Is Required to
Make the Required Revisions Within 24 Hours.
One “benefit” of written criticism is that it can be done without the
copywriter being present. A piece of copy can be read and emended at the
critic’s leisure. The marked-up manuscript can then be sent to the copywriter for
the needed revisions.
This is not true with the Peer Review. The copywriter is required to
participate. Without his participation, the procedure doesn’t work.
The copywriter has three functions at the Peer Review:

---
<!-- Página 58 -->

1. He notes all the suggestions offered. He does this by marking up his copy in
ink and by tape recording all suggestions as a back up.
2. He is there to answer any questions of fact that arise.
3. He is there as a participant, assigning his own numerical ratings and
providing his own suggestions for improved copy.
The copywriter needs to be at the Peer Review to hear all the suggestions in
context. That way, if a suggestion is made that he doesn’t understand, he can ask
for clarification immediately. Trying to reconstruct the meaning of a suggestion
days or weeks after it was made is an exercise in futility.
Most important, the copywriter needs to be there so he can experience the
positive energy that’s generated when a group of people put their heads together
with a common purpose … and use that energy to fuel his revisions.
Rule #6. There Must Be at Least Four Participants … No More
Than Six.
It should be clear by now that the Peer Review has two main components:
The Ratings — numerical scores to determine how well the lead is likely
to do with the target audience.
The Suggestions — lines of copy that can improve the copy considerably
and give it a better chance of working.

I’ve said that Peer Reviews work best when there are at least four, but

1. A group of three people is too small to represent a sample of the target

audience. Six, we’ve found, is a good, reliable number. Five is okay. And
four will do in a pinch. But the ideal is six. To get numeric evaluations you
can trust, shoot for five or six people.
2. A group of three people is too small for any fast, high-level brainstorming.
Groups of four, five, or six people always tend to be better.
If six is perfect, wouldn’t 60 be 10 times better?
No. Countless psychological and business studies have demonstrated that,
in order to be effective, people must operate in groups that are limited in size.
In The Tipping Point, Malcolm Gladwell observes that there is a great deal
of historical and anthropological evidence to suggest that the maximum number
for this is 150 people. Gladwell also suggests that there seems to be a natural
limit for any working group. He cites numerous records of tribal groups,
exploration teams, military outfits, and so on — all of which have naturally
limited their own growth.

Any more than that, and the leader is unable to manage the group effectively.

When it comes to the creative part of the process — the part where copy
suggestions are given — it helps to have some writers present.
Let me explain.
Anyone in your company who has responded to a direct-marketing offer

one senior copywriter
one junior copywriter
one apprentice copywriter
one senior marketer
one junior marketer
one customer service person

probably been trained in the critical method, they will also be most likely to
violate the prohibitions. The marketers will often have helpful insights that the
copywriters lack. Likewise, people from customer service.
Initially, the copywriters will tend to make most of the suggestions. It’s up
to the leader to make sure that everyone participates.

There you have it. The six main rules that make the Peer Review process

The Lead Must Meet a Minimum Score or the Peer Review Is Aborted
When we first started doing Peer Reviews, we used to put all leads through
the process, even those that got very poor scores. What we found was that we
spent a lot of time trying to come up with better headlines and copy, but were
seldom successful. The reason we failed, we came to believe, was that the copy
we were working with was too thin on ideas to give us the fodder we needed for
improving it.
So we eventually established a cut-off point — a minimum score the lead
had to achieve to merit going forward with the PR.
As I’ve mentioned before, Jack Forde, one of our colleagues who was
instrumental in helping us formulate and refine the Peer Review, uses 2.5 as his
cut-off. He feels confident that if the lead scores a 2.6 or 2.7, he will be able to
help his PR group make it work.
I have been using 2.8 as my cut-off point. I don’t think it matters whether
the number you use is 2.5 or 2.8, but I do think you need to impose the
minimum-score rule.
If the lead scores below the minimum, the Peer Review is terminated. The
leader announces: “The lead did not achieve the 2.8 (or 2.5) minimum score
needed for us to continue. Does anyone have any ideas for a different lead for
this promotion?”
For the next five minutes, everyone can make comments, suggestions, or
criticisms. The purpose is to stimulate an idea for a new lead, one that might be
good enough to merit a Peer Review.
After this five-minute no-rules period, we let the copywriter go back to his
computer and try to come up with a fresh approach. But there is no requirement
for him to use any of the ideas that were suggested. The only requirement is for
him to come back with copy that is stronger.
Be Aware of Influence
When asking for the numerical scores, the leader should ask the least
“powerful” participants first and the most powerful ones last. By powerful, I
mean the senior copywriters and marketers and anyone else whose suggestions

---
<!-- Página 62 -->

would likely influence the less-experienced members of the group.
Ask Low Graders to Offer Positive Suggestions
Anyone who rates the copy 2.8 or below is required to offer a suggestion.
This keeps people who happen to be in a bad mood that day from sabotaging the
Peer Review process by giving undeservedly low scores and contributing no
suggestions whatsoever. Sabotage rarely occurs, because the PR is such a
positive process. But when it does, the leader has to get the saboteur to cooperate
… or get rid of him.

The “CUB” Critique — A Simple Technique to Make Sure Your
Copy Gets Read
By Mike Palmer
________________________________
“The CUB process is a fast, effective, and easy way to
eliminate parts of copy that will impede the reader’s progress
through the copy. The highest value that I see, is that it easily
pinpoints these areas that the copywriter might miss through
numerous proofreadings and reviews himself because he is
so used to the copy he has written.”
— Will Newman, Copywriter
________________________________
As the head copywriter of one of the biggest financial newsletter businesses in
the world (Stansberry & Assoc. Investment Research), I’ve taught dozens of
brand-new copywriters over the past decade.
I can tell you from firsthand experience that Michael Masterson’s Peer
Review technique is probably the best copywriting secret in the world.
I know that’s a bold claim … and even in a world (and profession) filled
with hype, it’s not a claim I make lightly.

---
<!-- Página 65 -->

You see, Michael’s technique is one of the first things I teach new writers
… and is something we use EVERY SINGLE WEEK in my office of 14
copywriters.
If you learn how to use the Peer Review to evaluate the headline and lead of
every promotion, it will be impossible for you to send out bad copy, ever again. I
honestly believe it’s been worth at least $10 million to our business. We simply
don’t let bad copy get out the door.
But the Peer Review is just the first part of the Copy Logic system. Now
I’m going to teach you the second part, a technique that we developed in my
business.
It’s called the “CUB” Critique, and it has one simple purpose: to make sure
your reader never puts down your copy … until he’s read the entire promotion
and gets to the sale.
The CUB Critique assumes two things:
You’ve found an idea that is unique, compelling, and beneficial to your
reader. Your idea must “startle” your reader. It must be different than
anything he’s seen before. That’s the only way to write breakthrough copy.
If your ad sounds like an already existing ad, it will never be a blockbuster.
You’ve already used Michael’s Peer Review technique to create a great
headline and lead. Without a compelling headline and lead, the CUB
Critique is useless.
CUB, in essence, is a test to run on your copy when the entire promotion
has been written. It will not help you come up with a breakthrough idea. It will
not ensure that your reader is excited enough to buy. It will simply make sure he
does not put your copy down … once hooked with a good idea.
Think of your copy as a fishing lure.
The Peer Review makes sure you put a colorful spinning device in the
water that grabs the fish’s attention and gets him to take a bite. CUB is the barb
on the end of hook that makes sure he doesn’t let go.
So, let’s assume you’ve found a unique and beneficial idea to base your
promotion on. And let’s say the copywriter of that promotion (you or someone
you’ve hired) has written a compelling headline … and several pages that give

---
<!-- Página 66 -->

enough proof and specific explanation so that your reader knows this is
something new, real, and different than what he’s seen before.
Now … your reader is on the hook.
And you just have to make sure he doesn’t get loose.
Let’s see how CUB can make sure your copy gets read all the way through
to the order form.
3 Things That Will Make Your Reader Stop
I’ve found that there are three things that will make your reader stop
reading your copy after he’s already been hooked by the lead.
These “stoppers” are:
1. Confusing copy
2. Unbelievable copy
3. Boring copy
Confusing … Unbelievable … and Boring. That’s where “CUB” comes
from.
As I said, the CUB Critique has one purpose: to make sure you never give
your reader an excuse to stop reading.
What you have to remember is that your reader always has something better
to do. He is ALWAYS, ALWAYS looking for an excuse to throw your copy in
the trash (whether it’s e-mail or regular mail).
If he can trash it, that means there’s one less thing in his life he has to deal
with, so he can get back to reading the newspaper, watching TV, fixing dinner,
whatever.
CUB makes sure you never give your reader the excuse he needs to get
back to his hectic life.
I tell new copywriters to think about it this way …
Imagine that your reader has just come home from a day at the office … or
has just returned home after a trip to the grocery store. Picture yourself in that
scenario … think about your state of mind.

---
<!-- Página 67 -->

On the way into the house, you grab your mail and sift through it. Most
people I know create two piles — the pile to keep and the pile to chuck. Some
folks, like me, actually sort and read their mail standing directly over the trash
can.
Let’s assume your copy makes it into the “keeper” pile — which probably
less than 10 percent of junk mail does. What happens next?
Well, he opens the envelope, reads your headline and the first page or two.
If the idea isn’t of interest, he trashes it immediately … which about nine out of
the 10 people who open your envelope almost certainly do.
So what are you left with?
A tiny percentage of people who are going to actually read the promotion
you spent months carefully crafting.
And get this: The entire time he’s reading, he’s looking for an excuse to put
it down. But your headline intrigued him, so he had to keep reading. Then the
first few pages explained a little more, and offered some proof that this
opportunity is real … so he kept going.
Now he’s several pages into your copy — but still he’s looking for a good
reason to put it down. And the three reasons that will make him stop is copy that
is Confusing, Unbelievable, or Boring. (Bill Bonner, one of my mentors, who’s
also one of the world’s best copywriters of the past 25 years, has suggested that
we add a “D” to the CUB technique … to identify copy that is “Distracting.” But
to me that fits in the Confusing category.)
So how do you identify and fix these “stoppers”?
Let’s start with how the CUB Critique works … then I’ll explain what to do
to fix these problematic pieces of copy.
How to Perform the Cub Critique
The CUB Critique is similar to Michael Masterson’s Peer Review to
evaluate the headline and lead in one important respect: You don’t need
copywriting expertise to make it work.
You simply need a handful of readers who have at least a passing interest in
the topic your promotion is about.
I always look for a group of readers across the spectrum. (Remember, I

---
<!-- Página 68 -->

work mostly in the financial markets — but this holds true for all types of copy.)
On one end of the spectrum, I get a few of the very best copywriters in my
business. On the other end, my wife. She’s a very smart woman who has an
interest in the markets, but she’s never been taught the techniques of copywriting
and doesn’t read The Wall Street Journal.
One nice thing about the CUB Critique is that there’s no need for these
people to get together as a group. It can all be done by e-mail. You simply find
four or five people who are willing to read your copy closely, then send them
your file with instructions on what to do.
Here’s what I typically say:
Dear George —
I’ve just finished a new promotion. I’m wondering if you can do
me a favor.
I really value your opinion, and I’m wondering if you can read
this for me.
Specifically, I’d like you to look for sections of the copy that are:
1) Confusing
2) Unbelievable
3) Boring
If you could simply mark any of these sections, and then just jot a
note in the margin letting me know whether it was Confusing,
Unbelievable, or Boring, I’d really appreciate it. You have a great
eye for this type of thing, and I know my promotion will be
stronger if you can take the time to read it.
Of course, any other comments or suggestions you have are much
appreciated, but the main thing I’m looking for are places that are
Confusing, Unbelievable, and Boring.
Thanks so much,
Mike
Two things to keep in mind when asking someone to perform the CUB
Critique on your copy.

<!-- Página 69 -->

Flattery will get you everywhere. Now, of course, you should be sincere
about it, but don’t be afraid to let your CUB reader know you value his
opinion. Even the richest and most successful people in the world love to be
flattered.
It’s fine to have your reader mark up other things, in addition to the sections
that are Confusing, Unbelievable, or Boring. In fact, with a red pen in hand
or the “Track Changes” tool opened up in Microsoft Word, most people
can’t help giving you all kinds of extra feedback. Everyone loves to be a
critic.
You should take these extra criticisms with a grain of salt. If you think your
CUB reader knows what he’s talking about, you might follow a suggestion —
but most of the time you can skip the extra comments and just look at the places
they’ve marked as Confusing, Unbelievable, and Boring.
Let’s say you’ve sent your copy to four or five readers and have asked them
to perform the CUB Critique. They’ve sent back your file with a bunch of marks
on it.
What do you do next?
Well, you need to fix the sections that are marked. Let’s look at some real-
world examples and see how you make the corrections …

6
How to Eliminate Confusing Copy
By Mike Palmer
________________________________
“Like most good copy formulas, 90% of the copy I read
violates one or more of its precepts — proving the need
to learn and master CUB.”
— Bob Bly, author and freelance copywriter
________________________________
The first thing we ask people performing the CUB Critique to do is mark any
part of the copy that is Confusing. There are essentially four things that make
copy Confusing:
1. Esoteric Points. In other words, too much detail about something no one
cares about except you.
When you are working on a piece of copy you are VERY close to the
subject. You’ve likely spent a month or more talking to experts … and reading
books, websites, and dozens of mainstream media articles. So you know A LOT
more than your reader. The trick is to give your reader as much information as
possible, without boring him.

---
<!-- Página 71 -->

1. Confusing Language or Numbers. This is typically due to either lazy
work or just poor choices (of words, graphics, or numbers).
2. Vague Explanations. Not being specific enough is often Confusing.
3. Distractions. Great copy contains one strong idea, followed by page after
page of explaining and proving that idea in every way possible, so it’s
impossible for the reader to doubt what you’ve said. Readers get confused
when you veer too far off topic.
Remember, every time your reader is confused, his mind starts to wonder
… he begins thinking about what’s for dinner … or what he should be doing
instead of reading junk mail. Confusing copy makes it easy for him to put down
your copy for good.
You’ve got him hooked with an intriguing headline and lead — don’t blow
it with Confusing copy in the rest of the promotion.
The good news is that Confusing copy is very easy to fix. My favorite
solution is to simply cut it out. Always try that first. If you can cut it without
hurting the promotion, do so. If not, rewrite. In short, the solutions to Confusing
copy are: Cut it down … make it more specific … and simplify.
Let’s look at some real-world examples of Confusing copy that I’ve marked
over the past few years so you can see what I mean …
Confusing Word Choice
Here’s an example from a blockbuster promotion that sold about $1.5
million worth of subscriptions to a $99 investment newsletter in 2007. The
original copy read:
The bottom line is: If you’re at or near retirement age, and feel
like your retirement savings could use a boost, this investment
may be perfect for you.
Remember: All you have to do is enter … and you’ve got a good
chance to walk away with a huge payday. By the way, you can
enter as many times as you wish. (I’ll show you how.)
What’s Confusing and how to fix it: In the second paragraph, the writer
describes a way to “enter” this investment. He did that to make it sound different

---
<!-- Página 72 -->

from an ordinary investment — but here it’s just Confusing. It brings to mind a
slew of questions I never had before. The only real solution here is to cut the
entire second paragraph.
Confusing Numbers
When you use too many numbers, your reader’s eyes start to glaze over.
Here are two examples. The first from a promotion about the “Next Generation”
Internet … the second from a promotion about silver mining companies …
Example #1: And in regards to future Internet use, Security Soft
indicates that “87% of U.S. teens ages 12 to 17 currently use the
Internet, representing about 21 million youth, 11 million of which
go online on a daily basis.”
What’s Confusing and how to fix it: Five numbers is way too many in one
sentence. Instead, say: 87% of U.S. teenagers use the Internet … and more than
half of them go online, every single day.
Example #2: You see, whereas gold is hoarded purely for
investment purposes, silver has many industrial functions as well.
So, although gold is rarer inside Earth’s crust, most mined gold
stays above ground forever. Mined silver, on the other hand, is
continuously being depleted. There are around 4 billion ounces of
gold in global inventories, according to the U.S. Geological
Survey. But there’s 10 times less silver above ground.
What’s Confusing and how to fix it: How can you have “10 times less” of
something? You can have 50% less … or even 80% or 90% less. So instead, the
last two sentences should read: There are around 4 billion ounces of gold in
global inventories, according to the U.S. Geological Survey. But there’s 10% as
much silver above ground.
Vague Language That Gets Confusing
Very often, copywriters are not specific because they want the copy to have
mystery and suspense. The danger is that this will frustrate and confuse your
reader, and he will stop reading. Here’s a real live example of what I mean:
In 2002, members of Congress and President Bush began
formulating a new economic bill to benefit a certain group of

<!-- Página 73 -->

investments.
Investments which also happened to be owned by many of those
same members of Congress.
Consulting with them was Dr. John Rutledge. A world-famous
economist who has worked for several U.S. Presidents. In short,
Rutledge found that their proposed law would:
“Be the biggest event to hit the asset markets since 1981 … Have
a huge impact on asset prices, interest rates, growth, and the
dollar … Create a host of opportunities for investors to make
money and … Create a wave of restructuring, recapitalization,
and acquisition events among U.S. companies.”
What’s Confusing and how to fix it: Here, the copywriter didn’t name the
bill, the investment he’s talking about, or the law … so it turns into a garbled
mess. To fix it, simplify the copy and the point you’re trying to make. Save the
study for another section, after you explain what’s going on:
In 2002, members of Congress quietly passed a bill to help
themselves.
You see, most people don’t know that while Senators and
Representatives may have their differences, they almost all like to
get rich on the exact same investment.
So they passed a bill to make this secret Congressional
investment even more profitable than it was before. Here’s what I
mean …
Confusing Graphics
Charts and other graphics, used sparingly, are great for all types of copy,
not just financial newsletter promotions. Someone flipping through your
promotion may not read every word, but he will definitely look at your graphics.
The secret is to make them simple and easy to understand. Here are two that are
Confusing:

options play that made 113% gains in less than 4 months …
enough to pocket $11,300 in profits on a $10,000 stake — even as
the stock did absolutely NOTHING. Take a look …

What’s Confusing and how to fix it: The chart says the stock “went
nowhere.” Does that look like “nowhere” to you? Looks like huge moves up and
down, right? BETTER: Say the options went up 113% … while the stock price
moved violently up and down.
Example #2. This chart shows you what kind of profits I’m
talking about if you follow my recommendations:
What’s Confusing and how to fix it: This graphic is Confusing on many
levels. First, I have no idea what the writer is trying to say. Second, the gains
from the stocks look like they are getting smaller. Is that what he wants the

<!-- Página 75 -->

reader to take away? SOLUTION: Redo this chart completely, or simply make a
bulleted list with percentage gains.
Too Many Ideas at Once
One thing to remember is that your reader will only take away one idea
from your promotion. Great copy is one really powerful and unique idea …
simply explained and proven beyond any reasonable doubt. Many copywriters
try to throw in way too many ideas, with disastrous results. For example …
The companies that make up the “Oil & Gas Prosperity Program”
can be tough to find. After all, not many people know about them.
But I’ve spent the past year (including three trips to Canada)
researching the 6 very best participating businesses, which pay
you the fattest and most frequent dividend checks.
All 6 companies play a vital role in the oil and gas industry. In
fact, they’re an integral part of a very recent development in the
Canadian oil sector — one that has industry experts tripping over
themselves in excitement …
In fact, according to Tom Harding of the University of Calgary,
this breakthrough is “maybe even bigger than the railway.”
Let me give you the full details …
The story no one is telling …
The explosion of interest in the oil sands region is familiar to
investors.
As you may know, the capital of the oil sands frenzy is a town
called Fort McMurray, located north of nowhere.
Trapped beneath the frozen tundra lies seven times more oil than
Saudi Arabia’s proven reserves … enough oil to last us 4,896
years.
What’s Confusing and how to fix it: Confused? I am. Here, the writer has
so many ideas going on that it’s incredibly tough to follow. First there’s the Oil
and Gas Prosperity Plan, made up of six companies. Then there’s the idea of big
dividend checks. Then the idea that there’s a recent development in Canada’s oil
sector. Then the idea of oil sands … and a small town called Fort McMurray …

<!-- Página 76 -->

and oil trapped beneath the frozen tundra. Remember, your reader, no matter
how smart, can process only one idea at a time.
One way to fix it is to simply cut some of the ideas and tighten it up:
The companies that make up the “Oil & Gas Prosperity Program”
can be tough to find. After all, not many people know about them.
You have to go to a Canadian town called Fort McMurray to get
the full story.
Well, I’ve spent the past year (including three trips to the area)
researching the 6 very best participating businesses, which pay
you the fattest and most frequent dividend checks.
All 6 companies play a vital role in getting this oil and gas out of
the ground. (Keep in mind, there’s 7 times more oil here than in
Saudi Arabia.)
Much easier to follow, don’t you think?

7
How to Get Rid of Unbelievable Copy
By Mike Palmer
________________________________
“As copywriters, I think we tend to spend so much time
coming up with a great headline and lead that we sometimes
forget it’s possible to lose a sale later in the letter. The ‘CUB’
process lets me know the rest of my letter is just a strong as
my headline and lead.”
— Paul Hollingshead, Copywriter and Co-Founder of AWAI
________________________________
The second thing we ask our CUB critics to do is mark any sentence, statement,
claim, or promise in the copy that’s Unbelievable.
I tell them to be hypercritical. In other words, to read the copy with as
skeptical an eye as possible. Your reader in the real world will have a very alert
“B.S. meter.” He’s seen hundreds of similar marketing pieces before.
Tell your critics: “Pretend you are a crotchety old man who never believes a
word anyone says. Question everything.”
Unbelievable copy is a great reason for your reader to put down your
promotion. Maybe he’ll let you get away with something he doesn’t believe once

---
<!-- Página 78 -->

… but if he doubts you twice or more, your package is probably going into the
garbage.
How do you fix copy that has been marked Unbelievable?
The two best solutions are to (1) cut it or (2) prove it. A distant third
solution is to have the “narrator” acknowledge that the claim is unbelievable —
but you can typically do that only toward the end of the promotion … and you
should not do it more than once.
For example, you can say something like: “I know this sounds unbelievable
… and I wouldn’t believe it myself unless I had seen it with my own eyes … but,
etc.”
Remember, this is your last choice. It’s always better to cut or prove any
Unbelievable statement.
So let’s look at a few real-world examples of some of the Unbelievable
copy I’ve marked up over the past few years … and some easy ways to fix the
problem.
Unbelievable Claim
This is a section of copy about a $5,000-a-year specialty micro-cap, biotech
investment advisory …
We pay a premium for Rob and George’s specialized knowledge
— and we spend a fortune sending them around the world to
investigate companies firsthand. This is why Phase 1 Investor is
our most expensive and exclusive research service.
What’s Unbelievable and how to fix it: The claim about “spending a
fortune sending them around the world to investigate companies firsthand” is an
example of the type of lazy copy you see in many promotions. And it’s so easy
to fix. All you need is a very short list of the places they’ve been over the past
six months to prove the point. Be specific. When you are vague, it is almost
always Unbelievable. Remember, your reader is not stupid. He knows you are
trying to sell him something. So you absolutely must be specific at all times.
For example, you could say:
We pay a premium for Rob and George’s specialized knowledge
— and we spend a fortune sending them around the world to

---
<!-- Página 79 -->

investigate companies firsthand. In just the past 6 months, for
example, we’ve sent these guys to Singapore, Switzerland,
Belgium, Vancouver, San Diego, Boston, Dallas, and New York
— each time in pursuit of a new investment idea.
This extensive on-the-ground research costs us more than
$100,000 a year, and is why Phase 1 Investor is our most
expensive and exclusive research service.
Not Enough Specific Proof
Here’s a great example of a section of copy that is mediocre … but can
easily be made much stronger by making it more believable …
You see, big gold companies — companies that explore and
produce gold — take on all kinds of risk. They spend tremendous
amounts of money identifying a target gold deposit … exploring
it … developing it … and building the infrastructure to mine it.
What’s Unbelievable and how to fix it: Not too bad, right? But look how
much stronger the copy gets when you prove the point with specifics, simply by
adding two short paragraphs beneath it. Not only is the reader more likely to
believe the claim … you also give yourself instant credibility, because it shows
you understand how the industry works and have the connections to get these
facts.
For example, one of the most popular mining trucks today costs a
whopping $2.7 MILLION. Each tire costs $30,000 … and the
annual fuel costs run more than $1 million per truck!
Plus, I haven’t even mentioned the costs of labor … insurance …
permits … storage … and the hundreds of other pieces of super-
expensive equipment you need to operate a mine.
Unbelievable Clichés
You’ve got to work very hard in your copy to remove as many of the “sales”
and copy clichés as possible. Why? Because when your reader comes across a
cliché he’s heard in other marketing pieces, it reminds him of the fact that this is
a sales letter, and it makes him think your product is no different than others he’s
tried before. Here’s a cliché that appears in many financial newsletter

---
<!-- Página 80 -->

promotions …
Again, I think that’s a bargain, considering a small investment in
just one of the opportunities you read about each month in Phase
1 Investor could pay for your subscription … many times over.
What’s Unbelievable and how to fix it: This cliché is not terrible, but it’s
certainly overused. You’d be much better off showing a testimonial from a
skeptical buyer … who was able to more than pay for his subscription from the
gains he made. Or use the newsletter’s track record to show that the reader can
make money from the very beginning, so it actually won’t cost him anything.
For example:
Is Phase 1 worth the price? Well, you’ll have to judge for
yourself, but we had a similar question posted recently on our
members-only message board.
Ron Shepard, from St. Paul, MN, responded: “I’d never even
dreamed of spending $5,000 per year for an investment newsletter
… but I have to say, it’s worth every penny. I’ve made my money
back, and then some. Phase 1 might turn out to be the best
bargain I’ve ever had!”
Always fight hard to get rid of copy clichés in your sales letters.
Not Enough Facts
Here’s an example of a claim that’s not backed up by any proof whatsoever.
In short, every Fortune 500 company, small business, college, and
non-profit organization will have to convert to the Next
Generation Internet over the next few years. That’s why Forbes
calls it a $10 trillion industry over the next decade.
What’s Unbelievable and how to fix it: This statement may be true, but
why should a reader believe it? There are many possible solutions. You could
show how one or two Fortune 500 companies are already making the transition.
Or you could include a quote from a major newspaper that essentially makes the
same point. Also, notice how even the quote from Forbes is not believable,
because the writer didn’t put anything in quotation marks. We don’t know what
the magazine actually said.

A simple fix:
In short, every Fortune 500 company, small business, college, and
non-profit organization will have to convert to the “Next
Generation Internet” over the next few years. Many have already
done so, including: Microsoft, Home Depot, Apple, Best Buy,
IBM, Exxon, GM, Walmart, and AT&T. That’s why Forbes calls
it a “$10 trillion industry over the next decade.”
Remember, if you’ve got a good idea to sell, the #1 reason your reader
won’t buy is because he doesn’t believe you. Prove everything. You can always
go back during your final edits and take out some of your proof if you’ve got too
much.
Unbelievable Explanation
In almost every type of direct-mail promotion, one of your reader’s
objections will be: “If this is so good, why haven’t I heard of it before?” As a
general rule, you should try to answer this objection somewhere in your copy.
But the explanation needs to be good … and believable. Here’s one that’s not. It’s
in a promotion about how much oil is located in Saskatchewan’s oil sands
region, which is right next to the well-known Alberta oil sands region.
The question is, if Saskatchewan’s oil sands are so rich, then why
hasn’t anyone tapped them yet?
Well the answer is, quite simply, that no one ever thought to look
there!
What’s Unbelievable and how to fix it: Is anyone going to believe that a
huge oil province, which is located right next to one of the biggest oil
discoveries in the world, is just sitting there untapped … because no one thought
to look there? I seriously doubt it. To fix it, we simply moved up a piece of copy
that appeared later in the letter and offered a much more plausible explanation:
“The Saskatchewan government only recently put into place the
regulatory framework and workforce infrastructure that enables
companies to get the oil out.”
The original explanation — that no one thought to look there — should
simply be cut. And I think that’s an important point. Sometimes you might have

<!-- Página 82 -->

to cut things from your package even if they are true … just because they sound
so unbelievable.
A String of Unbelievable Claims
Here’s a good example of a section of copy that is Unbelievable on many
levels. In fact, there’s one Unbelievable claim after the next. You’ve got to work
hard get this kind of copy out of your promotions…
I’ve found two companies in the stock market — which have so
much cash on hand that even if they stopped doing business today
you could still make a profit as a shareholder. With solid stocks
like these — if worse came to worst, the two companies would
simply buy themselves, and you could still make a profit.
But let me emphasize: These stocks won’t remain cheap for long.
You’ll make the best and safest gains if you get in now …
You won’t have opportunities like this for at least another 10 or
20 years. These are the kind of stocks that you can buy now, in
very large quantities, and bank on for the rest of your life. I
expect the long-term average annual gains from both of these
positions will be above 20% for the life of your investment. You
just can’t do any better than that.
What’s Unbelievable and how to fix it: This is a disastrous section of
copy that needs to but cut or proven, point by point. As a reader, I certainly don’t
believe I can make money if these companies stopped doing business. Show me
an example. I also don’t believe these companies would “buy themselves.” What
does that even mean? Again, show me an example. I don’t believe they are going
to remain cheap for long … or that I won’t have this opportunity for another 10
or 20 years … or that I can possibly make 20 percent a year for the rest of my
life.
Here’s one way to fix it:
I’ve found one company in the stock market — which is so asset-
rich that even if they stopped doing business today, and the
company had to be sold off in parts, you could still make a profit
as a shareholder.
Remember, this company owns about $500 million worth of

<!-- Página 83 -->

beachfront real estate … yet it’s valued on the stock market at just
$250 million! You may remember a similar situation about five
years ago … a company called Donnely Holdings decided to split
up and sell off its parts, including lots of valuable real estate.
Shareholders walked away with 120% gains, overnight!
If you wait, I can almost guarantee you will miss this opportunity.
Remember, this company has been this cheap only once in its 50-
year history. And soon after it got this cheap, it went on a 2-year
run in which investors made 357%. You have that chance again
now … but it won’t last long.
The important point here is that your reader, from the very first lines, is
looking for a chance to dismiss your claims as Unbelievable. It’s a great reason
for him to stop reading.
So you’ve got to be hypercritical of your copy. And you’ve got to have
other people go through it closely and mark up every section that is
Unbelievable.

How to Get Rid of Boring Copy
By Mike Palmer
The third and final part of the CUB Critique is to get rid of Boring copy. No
matter how good your headline and lead is, boring copy will make your reader
put down your letter and get back to the real world.
There’s simply no reason to send out a promotion with Boring sections of
copy … because it’s such an easy thing to fix.
The good news is that the CUB Critique will help you identify Boring
sections quickly and easily. And then all you have to do to fix it is just cut it out
completely … or cut it way, way down.
There are several things a copywriter will do that will bore the reader to the
point of putting the copy down. Let’s look at examples of a few of these, which
I’ve taken from early versions of several promotions I’ve edited in recent years
…
Boring the Reader With What He Already Knows
A classic copywriting mistake is to go on and on about a problem your
reader already knows exists. At most, what you need to do is remind him of the
problem. Then focus most of the copy on the opportunity and the solution. For

---
<!-- Página 85 -->

example …
Today’s financial climate is cause for any investor to be worried
…
With the dollar plummeting, oil prices hitting record highs, and
the housing market in a slump, it’s clear that the 5-year-old bull
market has come to an end.
The Dow Jones industrial is down more than 1,100 points since
the start of the year. The Standard & Poor’s 500 is almost 15% off
its most recent high. And key blue-chip indexes are now down
nearly 10% from their recent record highs …
Even indexes of smaller stocks are down almost 14%.
On top of that, this is an election year. And the uncertainty that
every election brings is often an obstruction to U.S. market
performance.
This means that unless you’re one of those rare Americans whose
savings are tucked away in a foreign bank, you’ll likely lose
money over the next few years, if you haven’t already …
What’s Boring and how to fix it: If you are writing to someone has
cancer, you don’t have to tell him how bad cancer is. If you are writing to
someone who needs a job, you don’t have to tell him how bad it is to be
unemployed. If you are writing to an investor, you don’t have to go on and on
about how bad the markets are … he knows how much money he’s lost and how
terrible it is. What you need to do is tell the reader not to worry … there’s a
solution.
The above copy should be cut entirely … or perhaps cut down to a sentence
or two, such as:
The financial markets are a mess right now. Stocks, bonds, real
estate … everything is falling.
But the good news is, there’s an extremely safe way for you to get
extra income right now, which very few Americans have
considered …
Spending Too Much Time on a Boring Subject

You must act as an “excitement filter” for your copy. Meaning, you can’t
allow Boring subjects to linger. What kinds of subjects are guaranteed to be
Boring? Well, tax regulations … how the U.S. healthcare system works …
general legal principles, etc. Even if your promotion has something to do with
one of these things, you must filter out the complexities to make sure your reader
does not get bored. For example, here’s a section of copy from a recent
promotion we ran about the U.S. healthcare industry. The overall copy was very
strong, but here the writer got bogged down in minutiae.
No wonder the St. Louis Post-Dispatch calls these Pharmacy
Benefit Manager (PBM) companies “the most mystifying actors
in the healthcare system … that make hundreds of millions of
dollars a year …”
Of course, Express Scripts is probably the most well-known
independent PBM.
Many pharmacies themselves operate PBMs … such as
Pharmacare (owned by CVS) and Walgreens Health Services
(owned by Walgreens).
Or they can be part of larger managed care organizations, such as
Prescription Solutions (a subsidiary of UnitedHealth Group).
But many of the other independent PBMs are privately held …
and fairly secretive …
You won’t find much information on companies like 4D
Pharmacy Management Systems, Argus, Benecard Services,
Emdeon, Innoviant, and WellDyne … just to name a few.
What’s Boring and how to fix it: This copy simply needs to be cut down
and simplified. The writer is making an interesting point here — that every time
you buy a drug, there’s a secret middleman that makes a huge profit. But too
many details make this Confusing and Boring. Here’s one fix:
Did you know that every time you buy a drug, there’s a
middleman, called a Pharmacy Benefit Manager (or “PBM”) that
makes a huge profit?
Even when you buy from big drug discounters like Walgreens or
Rite Aid … the PBM middleman gets a huge cut.

<!-- Página 87 -->

Most of these PBMs are privately held firms (like Argus,
Benecard Services, Emdeon, Innoviant, and WellDyne … just to
name a few), but a few are publicly traded … and might be the
safest way to make money in America over the coming years …
Boring Copy That Drags
When reviewing your copy, you have to always keep in mind that there are a
dozen or more other things competing for your reader’s attention. You simply
can’t let the copy drag. You can’t let his eyes glaze over. The copy has to get to
the point, and fast. Here’s a good example of copy that is simply wandering,
taking far too long to get to the point …
My goal in Vancouver was to find the best new mining companies
(readers who took advantage of the 398% and 145% gains we
made on our last small mining recommendations understand why)
…
But instead of finding a new mining company, Rick introduced
me to the kind of super-small, unknown stock you would only
ever hear about from a true “industry insider” like him …
someone with all the right connections … someone at the center
of the action.
I can almost guarantee you’ve never heard of this stock.
It’s far too small, unfortunately, to recommend to my True Wealth
or DailyWealth readers …
But it’s perfect for a much smaller group.
Normally, I would reserve a recommendation like this for readers
of my most exclusive research service, Sjuggerud Confidential …
but I’m writing today to show you how to receive my research on
this situation, basically free of charge.
You see, I have just completed a new Research Report with all the
details on the small secret “insider” company I discovered in
Vancouver.
In my new report, you’ll receive all the details on this company
— where to buy it, who to call, and when to sell.
But first, I want to show you why Rick compares this company

<!-- Página 88 -->

(which he calls “the best business in the world”) to a similar
business whose shares went up 525% … and why the small stock
I recommend you buy right now could possibly return even more
than that in the next several years.
What’s Boring and how to fix it: You’d have to be a very patient reader to
stick with this copy. No one cares about which service it would be recommended
in … or what the writer was looking for … or who told him about it. This copy
wanders all over the place, boring and confusing the reader. It needs to be cut
down to about one paragraph.
Here’s one solution:
I recently went to Vancouver (the gold and silver mining capital
of the world) to do research on opportunities in this sector.

The Four-Legged Stool Test
By Michael Masterson
The Peer Review will help you create a very strong headline and lead. The CUB
Critique will eliminate three of the most common weaknesses in writing. The
combination of the Peer Review and the CUB Critique will drastically improve
your copy.
Now let’s look at the third technique in the Copy Logic system — a quick
and simple one — that will ensure that your sales message contains the four
essential components of the best direct-response copy.
The Four-Legged Stool: A History
The Four-Legged Stool was developed in the early 1990s at Agora, when
we asked ourselves one simple question: Why is it that our main competitor’s
promotions are beating ours time after time?
To find out, we collected every package they produced over a 12-month
period and spent a month studying them.
Were they making bigger or better promises than we were?
The answer was no. If anything, our promises were stronger than theirs.

---
<!-- Página 90 -->

Were they providing better proof than we were? Did they support their
claims better than we supported ours?
The answer, again, was no. Our packages were equal to theirs in proving
our claims.
Did their packages have bigger or better ideas than ours?
No. In fact, Agora’s copy was famous for its “big ideas.”
Finally, we noticed something they were doing that we weren’t doing at all.
They were taking pains to provide their gurus with credibility. They were
laying out their credentials: their schooling, their professional accomplishments,
the accolades they had garnered in their industry.
There was nothing in our copy that addressed credibility. We were so
focused on our ideas and promises and proof that we neglected it. We knew we
were credible. We knew our products were good. And so we supposed our
readers would somehow know that too. But they didn’t. And how could they, if
we didn’t provide them with evidence of it?
Could the lack of credibility be diminishing our response rates? We decided
to give it a test. We started inserting copy that demonstrated credibility in several
of our existing promotions.
The results were impressive. Immediately those packages started
performing better. Packages that were pulling in a one-half of one percent
response rate suddenly started pulling in three-quarters of a percent. And one-
percent packages were bringing in customers at a percent and a half.
We realize then that we had been trying to compete with a disadvantage. We
were balancing our promotions on three legs. Our competitor was balancing
theirs on four.
Thus we initiated what we called the Four-Legged Stool Test. A properly
built sales letter, we concluded, must contain four distinct and important
elements:
1. A big or unifying idea
2. A substantial promise of benefit supported by subordinate claims.
3. Ample proof for each of those claims
4. Evidence that the product, product provider, and person behind the sales

letter are all credible and trustworthy
We employed the Four-Legged Stool Test regularly thereafter. And, sure
enough, we continued to notice a distinct improvement in the response rates our
packages were getting.
Better packages mean more revenues and eventually more profit. We grew
quickly beyond the $50 million mark and were soon competing equally with our
main rival at $90 million to $100 million a year.
That growth was not entirely due to the use of this four-point checklist. But
it was an undeniable factor in that success.
Many years later, I am still using the Four-Legged Stool Test. I find it to be
a quick and easy way to detect potentially fatal problems in advertising copy.
Just this morning, for example, I used it to figure out what was wrong with an e-
mail promotion one of my clients had sent out. They had high hopes for the copy
because it was written by a talented new copywriter and because everyone who
read it thought it was strong. When it failed to get even a decent response, the
marketing team came to me and asked, “What the heck just happened?”
I didn’t take me long to figure it out.
The copy was based on a good idea and was full of promises and benefits. It
was also rich with claims and had plenty of proof. But one leg of the stool was
missing: There was nothing in the copy that said anything about the credentials
of the person who had authored the home-study program it was selling.
The author was a world-class expert — and yet there was no mention of his
qualifications!
“If you asked me who the number one expert on this subject is,” I told the
marketing team, “I’d say it was the guy who wrote this home-study program.
And yet the copy doesn’t mention his credentials. There’s nothing about how
great he is at teaching this critical success skill. No endorsements from other
professionals. No examples of how he’s helped people. And except for the
personal story from the person who is signing the letter, there are no testimonials
from people who’ve benefited from his advice.”
I pointed out how successful advertising packages for similar products were
very strong in providing credentials. “There is a reason these guys are doing
this,” I told them. “We should be doing it too.”

Then we went through the promotion, inch by inch, identifying where it

It’s understandable that the original version of this promotion was lacking
that one crucial element. It was written by a new copywriter, and new
copywriters tend to rely almost exclusively on the power of making big
promises. Big promises help, but they work much better when they are combined
with proof, credibility, and a unifying idea.
In teaching new copywriters Four-Legged Stool technique, we have found
that there is sometimes confusion about what we mean by “big” or “unifying”
idea or the difference between credibility and proof. To clear up any confusion, I
have provided examples of each leg below.
Once you have these concepts firmly in mind, you will be able to quickly
run through the four-point checklist when evaluating any advertising copy.
Copywriters can use it to make sure their copy is well balanced before they show
it to anyone. Marketers can use it to detect the weaknesses in copy that is given
to them.
The Four-Legged Stool Test is not meant to be incorporated in the Peer
Review or the CUB Critique. It is an analytical tool — something you should
use to assess a promotion that has already been written. It is easy to do. And it
can be a big help in avoiding disappointment.
The Four-Legged Stool Test won’t, in itself, guarantee breakthrough copy.
But it will make all your copy well balanced. And sometimes, as we found out,
having a well balanced promotion can make the difference between doing well
and doing very well.
First Leg: The Idea
Coming up with a big “tipping-point” idea is the single best way to create
breakthrough promotions.
Big tipping-point ideas not only capture your readers’ attention but signal to
them that they are going to learn something valuable by reading the rest of your
letter.
Big ideas trigger a “Yes! That’s so true!” type of response. They confirm
deep-seated beliefs your prospective customers have about the world, and appeal

to deep-seated desires they have about what kind of people they want to be.
Without a big idea, a promotion can work — and sometimes work well. But
the prospect may not be emotionally committed to the product because he won’t
understand how buying it will solve some bigger, life-changing problem.
Here is an example of what I mean by a tipping-point idea:
How the French Live Longer Than Everyone Else …
Even though they eat like kings and smoke like chimneys!
Tipping-Point Idea: French people live long healthy lives without dieting
or exercising or doing any of the other things U.S. doctors are always
recommending that we do.
How is that possible?
To readers concerned with health and longevity, this idea is compelling
because it offers to answer a riddle that has puzzled them for many years — why
French people who eat cheese and meat and rich sauces stay so thin — and
another one that they have just discovered: The French outlive them too!
Implicit in this tipping-point idea is a promise that will appeal to almost
anyone: that you can eat like a French person … and lose weight and live longer.
Tipping-point ideas always come equipped with appealing promises. That
accomplishes two tasks at once:
They involve the prospect’s mind and divert him from the impending sale.
They include an implicit promise that “hooks” the prospect emotionally.
Imagine this lead without a big idea. It would probably be something
terribly dull — like “How to Stay Healthier and Live Longer.”
